Post Hole FAQ

How deep should fence post holes be?

The general rule is 1/3 of the post length should be in the ground:

  • 1.8m fence (6ft): 600mm deep minimum
  • 2.1m fence (7ft): 700mm deep
  • 2.4m fence (8ft): 800mm deep

Minimum depth should never be less than 450mm for stability.

How wide should the hole be?

Hole diameter should be at least 3 times the post width:

  • 90mm post: 270mm minimum (300mm recommended)
  • 100mm post: 300mm minimum
  • 125mm post: 375mm minimum (400mm recommended)

This provides enough concrete around the post for proper anchoring.

How many bags per fence post?

Typical requirements for a 300mm diameter × 600mm deep hole:

  • Quick-set post mix (20kg bag): 1-2 bags per post
  • Standard concrete (20kg bag): 2-3 bags per post

Use our calculator above for exact quantities based on your hole size.

Should I use quick-set or standard concrete?

Quick-set post mix:

  • Sets in 20-40 minutes
  • Can attach fence panels same day
  • Slightly more expensive
  • Best for fence posts and letterboxes

Standard concrete:

  • 24-48 hours to set
  • More working time for adjustments
  • Stronger final result
  • Better for structural posts (decks, pergolas)

How Much Concrete for Fence Posts in Australia

Setting fence posts in concrete is one of the most common DIY concreting jobs in Australia. Getting the right amount of concrete ensures your fence stands strong for years, whether you're building a standard timber paling fence or a more substantial boundary fence.

Australian Fence Post Sizes

The most common timber fence post sizes in Australia are 90×90mm and 100×100mm for standard residential fencing. Steel posts, popular for their durability and termite resistance, typically come in 65×65mm sizes. For heavier-duty applications like front fences or dividing fences that need to support gates, 125×125mm timber posts provide extra strength.

Recommended Hole Dimensions

A good rule of thumb is to dig your post hole at least three times the width of the post. For a 100mm post, this means a hole diameter of 300mm. The depth should be approximately one-third of the total post length that will be above ground. For a standard 2.4 metre fence post where 1.8 metres is above ground, you would bury 600mm in concrete. Most Australian councils and fencing contractors recommend a minimum depth of 450mm for standard fences and 600mm for structural posts.

How the Calculation Works

This calculator determines the concrete volume by calculating the cylindrical volume of each hole and subtracting the volume occupied by the post itself. The formula uses π × radius² × depth for the hole volume, minus the post dimensions multiplied by the depth it extends into the concrete. This gives you an accurate figure for the actual concrete needed, not just the hole size.

Tips for Setting Posts in Concrete

Rapid-set concrete is ideal for fence posts because it sets quickly and eliminates the need for temporary bracing overnight. Some products, like Rapid Set Concrete Post Mix, can be poured dry into the hole around the post and simply watered. This makes the job much easier for DIYers working alone. For best results, place about 50mm of gravel at the bottom of each hole for drainage before adding concrete.

Council Regulations

Australian fencing regulations vary by state and local council. Boundary fences between neighbours are typically covered by state fencing acts, which specify height limits and cost-sharing arrangements. Front fences often have stricter height restrictions, usually limited to 1.2 metres without council approval. Corner properties may have visibility requirements that affect fence placement. Always check with your local council before building, especially for fences over 2 metres high or within certain distances of property boundaries.

Common Post Projects

Beyond standard fence posts, this calculator works for deck posts that need substantial footings, pergola posts that may require deeper embedment for structural stability, and letterbox posts. For structural applications like decks and pergolas, consider using larger posts and deeper holes than you might for a simple fence, and always check if your project requires engineering certification or a building permit.

Why add wastage?

Wastage accounts for concrete lost during your project:

  • Uneven ground requiring more concrete in low spots
  • Spillage during pouring and spreading
  • Concrete sticking to mixing equipment
  • Minor measurement variations
10% is standard for most DIY projects.
Use 15% for uneven sites or complex shapes.
